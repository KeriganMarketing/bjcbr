  On Call:
Has your hip been recalled??
— by William Sherman, M.D.

There have been several orthopedic implants recalled in the past ten years but none have received the attention of the Dupuy ASR hip replacement. There is a growing concern among patients about faulty implants and if they have received a "good" one or a "defective" one. 

The fact is that there are five major joint manufactures as there are five major truck manufacturers. Ford, Chevy, Toyota, GMC, and Dodge. Just as these trucks are all the same, they are all slightly different and each make has many different models and options. Each loyal customer will give detailed reasons as to why one truck is better to the other just as any orthopedic surgeon will give strong evidence the implant he/she uses for hip and knee replacements is superior to the others. The truth is the driver of the truck is more important than the vehicle itself assuming the trucks are somewhat comparable.

I use Toyota as an example to my patients when asked if their hip has been recalled.

Most people have not received the Dupuy ASR system, however,  a large percentage have received Dupuy hips and knees. I tell them about the 2008 Camry recall for a faulty gas pedal that was an enormous safety hazard and a true concern. It certainly would stop me from buying a Camry but would have no affect on me buying a truck from Toyota. While it is the same company, it is a totally different vehicle and Toyota trucks may be the most reliable long term on the market (insert argument here of any person from south Louisiana that drives a different make).

The fact is almost every major implant producer of hip and knee implants has recalled items, some on a larger scale than others. Dupuy’s new ASR has received much attention from attorneys and patients because of an unacceptable high failure rate. This system is recognizable on an xray, however, many other implants are very similar and can be confused for this implant.

The way we approach this currently is to first ask the patient if he/she is having any trouble with the hip.  If the answer is no, please have them see an orthopedist every 2-3 years for serial x-rays to ensure good wear patterns and detect any issues early.

If they are having any trouble with their hip, have them ask their surgeon what implant they received, or have their surgeon pull their implant record. The fact is that this recall affected 93,000 patients worldwide and of those roughly 85 percent are functioning well. Reassure them that they could have the same company's implant but a model that has been "on the road" for 20 years and has no issues at all. If you do discover that they have a Dupuy ASR system that is not functioning, have them see an orthopedist within a few weeks.

Did You Know?:
Anterior Approach to Total Hip Replacement
— by Lawrence Messina, M.D.

 
In recent years, minimally invasive surgery with a smaller incision has gained wide acceptance in all fields of medicine, and orthopedic surgery has certainly been no exception. Smaller incisions lead to shorter recovery and faster rehabilitation. As part of accelerated rehabilitation, the anterior approach to the hip, a technique performed since the 1940’s, has begun to have renewed enthusiasm. It allows for early mobilization in patients without restrictions. For example, limited range of motion in a seated position or getting off a commode because of the risk of dislocation allows patients an earlier return to functional activities.

Early total hip surgery was initially performed through a lateral approach to preserve the posterior capsule, thereby preventing hip dislocations. This approach necessitated an osteotomy of the greater trochanter which resulted in complication. The posterior approach was developed and popularized. As a result shorter surgical times and decreased blood loss made it very popular, but possible sciatic nerve injury and the risk of dislocation led some surgeons to an anterolateral approach to the hip joint. This, however, required detachment of the gluteus minimus muscle, a prime hip stabilizer, which then led to delayed rehabilitation. The anterior approach to the hip preserves the posterior structures preventing postoperative dislocation and thereby the restrictions on hip flexion which are required from the posterior approach. It also prevents disturbance of the gluteal muscles which allows for faster recovery of a normal gait. In performing the procedure from the anterior approach, what is done under the skin is important, not just the small incision. By not detaching muscles from the pelvis and femur and going around and between muscles and nerves, the patient enjoys the benefit of a procedure with decreased pain, very low risk of dislocation and associated precautions, hence, a faster recovery. Also because the procedure is done with x-ray control it is easier for the surgeon to re-approximate leg lengths with greater accuracy.

The final benefit of this procedure is patient selection. Whereas, most minimally invasive surgery is performed on thin or patients of normal build, patient selection sometimes prevents operative procedures which might otherwise be advantageous. In the hip joint, however, subcutaneous fat does not increase as much anteriorly around the hip joint as it does posteriorly. The procedure is actually easier as less subcutaneous fat has to be cut. This is important because the obese patient needs rapid recovery the most. This makes this procedure available for essentially all patients undergoing hip replacement.

Since performing this procedure, I and other surgeons at the Bone and Joint Clinic, have seen our patients enjoy faster recovery and decreased pain with earlier return to independence.

Sporting Goods:
Safe Pitch Count: Baseball Injury Prevention
With the start of the baseball season each spring, doctors frequently see an increase in elbow problems in young baseball players. A common elbow problem in these children is medial apophysitis, commonly referred to by doctors as "Little Leaguer's elbow."

"Little Leaguer's elbow" and osteochondritis dissecans affect pitchers and other players who throw repetitively.

Medial Apophysitis (Little Leaguer's Elbow)
This injury occurs when repetitive throwing creates an excessively strong pull on the tendons and ligaments of the elbow. The young player feels pain at the knobby bump on the inside of the elbow.

X-rays of the right elbow joint of an 8-year-old pitcher show irregularities (see arrows).
"Little Leaguer's elbow" can be serious if it becomes aggravated. Repeated pulling can tear ligaments and tendons away from the bone. The tearing may pull tiny bone fragments with it in the same way a plant takes soil with it when it is uprooted. This can disrupt normal bone growth, resulting in deformity.

Osteochondritis Dissecans
A less common condition called osteochondritis dissecans is also caused by excessive throwing, and may be the source of the pain on the outside of the elbow. Muscles work in pairs. In the elbow, if there is pulling on one side, there is pushing on the other side. As the elbow is compressed, the joint smashes immature bones together. This can loosen or fragment the bone and cartilage. The resulting condition is called osteochondritis dissecans.
Symptoms
"Little Leaguer's elbow" may cause pain on the inside of the elbow. A child should stop throwing if any of the following symptoms appear:
  • Elbow pain
  • Restricted range of motion
  • Locking of the elbow joint
Treatment & Prevention
Left untreated, throwing injuries in the elbow can become complicated conditions. Younger children tend to respond better to nonsurgical treatments like rest, applying ice, revising throwing technique and postponing activity.

Surgery is occasionally necessary, especially in girls older than 12 years and boys older than 14 years. Depending upon a child's injury, surgery may involve removing loose bone fragments, bone grafting, or reattaching a ligament back to the bone.

The general guideline for how many pitches a child can safely throw each week is 75 for 8-10 year olds, 100 for 11-12 year olds, and 125 for 13-14 year olds. This includes both practice and competitive play. To prevent throwing injuries, young pitchers should only play three to four innings each game.

Source: American Acedmy of Orthopedic Surgery.
