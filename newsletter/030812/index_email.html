  On Call:
Has your hip been recalled??
— by William Sherman, M.D.

There have been several orthopedic implants recalled in the past ten years but none have received the attention of the Dupuy ASR hip replacement. There is a growing concern among patients about faulty implants and if they have received a "good" one or a "defective" one. 

The fact is that there are five major joint manufactures as there are five major truck manufacturers. Ford, Chevy, Toyota, GMC, and Dodge. Just as these trucks are all the same, they are all slightly different and each make has many different models and options. Each loyal customer will give detailed reasons as to why one truck is better to the other just as any orthopedic surgeon will give strong evidence the implant he/she uses for hip and knee replacements is superior to the others. The truth is the driver of the truck is more important than the vehicle itself assuming the trucks are somewhat comparable.

I use Toyota as an example to my patients when asked if their hip has been recalled.

Anterior Approach to Total Hip Replacement
— by Lawrence Messina, M.D.

 
In recent years, minimally invasive surgery with a smaller incision has gained wide acceptance in all fields of medicine, and orthopedic surgery has certainly been no exception. Smaller incisions lead to shorter recovery and faster rehabilitation. As part of accelerated rehabilitation, the anterior approach to the hip, a technique performed since the 1940’s, has begun to have renewed enthusiasm. It allows for early mobilization in patients without restrictions. For example, limited range of motion in a seated position or getting off a commode because of the risk of dislocation allows patients an earlier return to functional activities. Read More >>

Safe Pitch Count: Baseball Injury Prevention
With the start of the baseball season each spring, doctors frequently see an increase in elbow problems in young baseball players. A common elbow problem in these children is medial apophysitis, commonly referred to by doctors as "Little Leaguer's elbow."

"Little Leaguer's elbow" and osteochondritis dissecans affect pitchers and other players who throw repetitively.

Medial Apophysitis (Little Leaguer's Elbow)
This injury occurs when repetitive throwing creates an excessively strong pull on the tendons and ligaments of the elbow. The young player feels pain at the knobby bump on the inside of the elbow.

X-rays of the right elbow joint of an 8-year-old pitcher show irregularities (see arrows).
"Little Leaguer's elbow" can be serious if it becomes aggravated. Repeated pulling can tear ligaments and tendons away from the bone. The tearing may pull tiny bone fragments with it in the same way a plant takes soil with it when it is uprooted. This can disrupt normal bone growth, resulting in deformity. Read more >>
