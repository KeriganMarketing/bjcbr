Sporting Goods:
Preventing injury in youth sports
— by Alan C. Schroeder, M.D

Kids love to play outside, and it doesn’t take long for them to become interested in competitive sports. Athletics are different than the “free play” that young children are used to, and as they grow older, teens can be tempted to push their bodies further than ever before, which can easily lead to injury. Here are some best practices to follow when they starts playing competitively.

  1. Warm up, stretch and cool down. Warm ups can help prevent common injuries like strained muscles and other soft tissue ailments. They should involve low-impact exercise such as jogging to raise the heart rate up from resting level. After warming up, always stretch. Teams should have a standard stretching exercise before each practice or game and should be supervised by the coach or team trainer so that stretching is done correctly. This will help prevent strains and increase flexibility. After activity, cool down properly. Just like warming up, the body needs to let heart rate gradually return to resting level. 

  2. Use proper technique. This is to, not only improve a player’s game, but also so they don’t hurt themselves unknowingly. Coaches and trainers know proper technique and should be present to teach it at practice. It’s also important to consult a physician about training programs, to make sure that they match up with current health and fitness level.

  3. Wear protective gear. Baseball requires the use of helmets for the team at bat. Soccer requires shin guards. More pads are required for football and not only should they always be worn during play, they should fit correctly. When pads don’t fit, they don’t protect the important areas of our body that they should, leaving risk for injury. Wearing helmets that don’t fit can lead to injuries like concussions. Per WebMD, in the last decade, the number of youth treated in hospital ERs for concussions they got while playing sports has doubled from roughly half a million in 2001 and is on the rise despite a decline in participation.

  4. Stop and rest. Some athletes, trainers and sports medicine physicians recommend against year-long athletic training or competitive sports without taking a season off, because muscles that are overused can become injured. Baseball requires heavy use of upper body and pitchers tend to throw out their arms. Researchers from the the American Sports Medicine Institute in Birmingham, AL say that young baseball players should pitch no more than 100 innings a year. In this study, players that exceeded the limit were more likely to undergo shoulder or elbow surgery later on in life and in some cases this happened before the early age of 15.

  5. Drink up. Having enough water in the body is essential for maintaining a healthy athletic body and performing well in sports. Dehydration can cause cramps and spasms. The best way to prevent that is to take a water break every 30 minutes. It’s also important to not drink too much at once, as this can cause discomfort. With increased activity and temperature, it is best to take breaks more frequently to avoid heat exhaustion.
