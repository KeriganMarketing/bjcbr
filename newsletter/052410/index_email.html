Sporting Goods:
- Alan C. Schroeder, M.D.

Summertime is fully underway and the afternoon temperatures are getting very high. Prepare yourself with adequate hydration and sun screen for outdoor activities. Ongoing fluid losses can be quiet high with moderately intense activities in the afternoon. Replace fluid loss in small increments to keep proper hydration and prevent cramps. Avoid starting a new exercise program during the high heat intensity portion of the day unless you are already conditioned to the heat.

High School football is getting underway with summertime workouts. These athletes can lose tremendous amounts of fluid due to the activity level, protective equipment worn, and multiple work outs in a single day. Oral hydration is a must to keep optimum performance and prevent cramping which is a symptom of dehydration. Later symptoms of heat overload can lead to serious conditions such as heat exhaustion, heat stroke or death.

Now, in the early season, is when athletes are most susceptible to injury. Light jogging followed by a “warm stretch” can decrease muscle strains. Acute traumatic injuries such knee ACL tears may occur from noncontact mechanisms, and shoulder labral tears may occur from forced arm rotation as in a dislocation. Overuse tendinitis and low back pain may occur from training that is too intense without necessary recovery time. Additional information can be obtained through your school coach, athletic trainer, physical therapist, physician or the AOSSM at www.STOPSportsInjuries.org.

What Is Pain, Exactly?
- Meredith Warner, M.D.

The word ‘pain’ derives most likely from the Latin ‘poena’ which means punishment or Greek ‘poine’ which is a word related to the concept of penalty. Pain is generally unpleasant and is a way for the body to signal that something is damaging. There are two classes of pain and two subsets. There is nociceptive pain, which may be visceral or somatic. There is non-nociceptive pain which is neuropathic or sympathetic. There are also two types of pain with regard to time. There is acute pain, which is short-lived and in response to injury. Usually, if the injury heals, the acute pain disappears. Chronic pain lasts longer and is not related to an injury. Pain can also be ‘referred’. In other words, you may be hurt in one spot but feel the pain elsewhere. Often, shoulder injuries are confused as neck pain for instance.

Nociceptive pain occurs when the sensory nerves that transmit pain are stimulated. This can happen with temperature (hot/cold), vibration, crush or stretch. Chemicals can also causes nociceptive pain. Pain during everyday life is typically somatic pain. read more >>
