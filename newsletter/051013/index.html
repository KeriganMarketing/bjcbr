On Call:
Welcome Dr. Michael Frierson

We proudly announce the association of Dr. Michael Frierson as he joins Bone & Joint Clinic of Baton Rouge.

Dr. Frierson attended the University of Michigan where he studied Microbiology. He earned his Medical Degree from the University of Michigan Medical School in Ann Arbor, Michigan in 1987. He completed an internship at Loyola University Medical Center in Maywood, Illinois in June of 1988. He also completed a residency at Loyola in 1992. He is a Pediatric Orthopaedic Specialist and completed a Fellowship at the Shriners Hospital for Crippled Children in Tampa, Florida in 1993.

Dr. Frierson became certified in orthopaedic surgery in 1996 by the American Board of Orthopaedic Surgery. For the past 18 years he has contributed his time to The McMains Children's Developmental Center and to Children's Special Health Services. Dr. Frierson has achieved recognition through the Army Achievement Medal for Superior Service as Staff Orthopaedic Surgeon and the Walter P. Blount Pioneer Award for research from the Pediatric Orthopaedic Society Meeting of North America.

Dr. Frierson is accepting new patients and appointments by calling Bone and Joint Clinic or by requesting an appointment online.

Scoliosis:
Early Detection is Key
By Dr. Michael Frierson

Scoliosis is the lateral bending and abnormal rotation of the spine. Curvature of the spine oft en occurs in the absence of other abnormalities or causes. Scoliosis is found to aff ect 1-2% of people.

Scoliosis is usually detected in early adolescence on routine physical exam by pediatricians or during school screenings. Girls are found to have scoliosis 8 times more oft en than boys. Inactive kids are not more likely to develop scoliosis. When adolescents are growing rapidly, their curve is more likely to increase in size. Pain is rarely a symptom associated with coliosis, even in children with a large curve. Carrying heavy books will not make scoliosis worse or more likely to occur.

Once the diagnosis is made, scoliosis is monitored with routine examinations and x-rays. Fortunately, the spine is not weakened by the deformity, and therefore, no restriction on activities or contact sports is necessary. Unfortunately, exercise, physical therapy or chiropractic manipulations are not eff ective ways of stopping curve progression.

Bracing is recommended for progressive curves over 20 degrees in a growing child. Customized braces have been shown to prevent scoliosis from getting worse and can sometimes prevent the need for surgical correction. Th e biggest reason for failure of the brace is patient compliance. Large curves over 40 degrees can lead to progressive deformity even aft er skeletal maturity. A small percentage of patients with scoliosis require surgical stabilization.

Early detection is the key. If bracing fails, surgical correction can be a predictably lasting solution. Th e good news is that most kids with scoliosis do not require any treatment at all other than observation by a pediatric orthopedist.
